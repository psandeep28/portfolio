Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Parth Sandeep — psandeep@ucsd.edu

Proposition: “Clinic closures have had a measurable effect on abortion rates between 2017 and 2020.”

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • (+2) Used a regression line to show overall trend. This helps communicate correlation to readers, even when individual points vary.
  • (+1) Axis-centered at zero to visually emphasize directional relationships (gain/loss in both variables).
  • (-1) Title frames the viewer to expect correlation (“More Clinic Closures, Lower Abortion Rates?”).

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • (+2) Z-score normalization allows for fair visual comparison of different scales between metrics.
  • (+1.5) Heatmap highlights lack of consistent positive or negative association across states, countering the idea of a universal pattern.
  • (0) Title uses cautious language: “No Clear Pattern,” avoiding heavy framing or bias.

Final Reflection

Designing both sides of a proposition helped me appreciate how much influence visualization design has on the interpretation of data. The most surprising part was how easy it was to guide viewer perception without manipulating the actual numbers. In the scatterplot, adding a regression line and centering the axes around zero subtly reinforced the idea of correlation between clinic closures and abortion rates, even though many data points deviated from the trend. That one design choice gave the chart rhetorical weight, which made it feel more persuasive even if the statistical correlation wasn’t particularly strong.

On the other hand, the heatmap, used for the counterargument, felt more neutral at first glance but was still quite powerful. By normalizing the data and showing all states, I was able to make the case that there is no consistent national pattern. The wide variation in values across states undermines the claim of a strong relationship between clinic availability and abortion rates. It helped me realize that even "neutral" design choices (like full data inclusion or normalization) carry rhetorical implications, sometimes strengthening the counterargument more effectively than a more obviously persuasive design.

Overall, this assignment pushed me to think more critically about ethical visualization. I now see that ethical analysis doesn’t mean avoiding persuasion altogether, it means being aware of how design choices affect interpretation and staying transparent about the intentions behind them. To me, the boundary between acceptable persuasion and misleading design lies in subtlety and transparency: if the audience is likely to come to a false conclusion based on a visual shortcut, the design may be crossing a line. Ethical visualizations should aim to inform and guide rather than manipulate, and this project clarified how easy it is to blur that line if we're not careful.
